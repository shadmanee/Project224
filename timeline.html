The History Of Bangladesh

  • Ancient and Classical Bengal

    Prehistory

    Following the Stone and the Copper ages, Bengal was settled by Austroasiatics, Tibeto-Burmans, Dravidians and Indo- Aryans in consecutive waves of migration. Archaeological evidence confirms that by the second millennium BCE, the Bengal delta was inhabited by rice-cultivating communities. Rivers such as the Ganges and Brahmaputra were used for transport while maritime trade flourished in the Bay of Bengal.

  • Iron Age

    The Iron Age saw the development of coinage, metal weapons, agriculture and irrigation. Large urban settlements formed in the middle of the first millennium BCE, when the Northern Black Polished Ware culture dominated the northern part of Indian subcontinent.

  • Mauryan Empire

    One of the largest empires in the subcontinent history, the Mauryan empire was established by Chandragupta Maurya. Under Mauryan rule, a single efficient system of finance, administration and security was created. The reign of Ashoka ushered an era of social harmony, religious transformation anf expansion of sciences and knowledge.

  • Gupta Empire

    The Gupta empire is regarded as a golden age in the subcontinental history. It was marked by extensive scientific and cultural advancements that crystallized the elements of what is generally known as the Hindu culture. Bengal was an important province under the Gupta empire. The discovery of Gupta coins across Bengal point to a monetized economy.

  • Magadha

    Ancient Bengal was often ruled by dynasties based in the Magadha region, such as the Shunga dynasty.

  • Gauda Kingdom

    King Shashanka is considered by some scholars to be the pioneering king of a unified Bengali state. Shashanka established a kingdom in the citadel of Gauda. The Bengali calendar traces its origin to Shashanka's reign.

  • Varman Dynasty

    The Varman dynasty of Kamarupa ruled North Bengal and the Sylhet region. The area was a melting pot of the Bengali-Assamese languages.

  • Khadga Dynasty

    The Khadga dynasty was a Buddhist dynasty of eastern Bengal. One of the legacies of the dynasty is its gold coinage inscribed with the names of rulers such as Rajabhata.

  • Pala Empire

    The Pala empire was a Bengali empire and the last Buddhist imperial power int the Indian subcontinent. The name Pala means protector. Gopala was the first ruler of the empire, coming to power through an election by chieftains in Gauda. The Pala dynasty lasted for four centuries and ushered in a period of stability and prosperity in Bengal. The empire reached its peak under Emperor Dharmapala and Devapala. The death of Devapala ended the period of ascendancy of the Pala dynasty and several independent dynasties and kingdoms emerged at this time.

  • Chandra Dynasty

    The Chandra dynasty ruled the southeastern Bengal and Arakan between the 10th and 11th centuries CE. The Chandra kingdom covered the Harikela region. The dynasty's realm was a bridge between India and southeast Asia. During this period, the port of Chittagong developed banking and shipping industries. The last ruler of the Chandra dynasty, Govindachandra, was defeated by the south Indian emperor Rajendra Chola I of the maritime Chola dynasty.

  • Sena Dynasty

    The Pala dynasty was replaced by the resurgent Hindu Sena dynasty which hailed from South India. The Sena dynasty brought about a revival of Hinduism and cultivated Sanskrit literature in eastern India.

  • Deva Dynasty

    The Deva dynasty was a Hindu dynasty of medieval Bengal that ruled over eastern bengal after the collapse of the Sena empire. The capital of this dynasty was Bikrampur in present-day Munshiganj district of Bangladesh. The Deva synasty endured after Muslim conquests, but eventually died out.

  • Medieval and Early Modern Bengal

    Delhi Sultanate

    (1204-1352)

    The Islamic conquest of Bengal began with the capture of Gauda from the Sena dynasty in 1204. Led to victory by Bakhtiar Khilji, the Delhi Sultanate maintained a strong vigil on Bengal. Gradually, eastern Bengal was absorbed into Muslim rule, through the Conquest of Sylhet. Sufis played an important role in the absorption fo Bengal. During the Tughluq dynasty, the taka was introduced as the imperial currency.

  • Small Sultanates

    (1338-1352)

    During the middle of the 14th century, three breakaway sultanates emerged int he Delhi Sultanate's province of Bengal. These included a realm led by Fakhruddin Mubarak Shah (and later his son) in Sonargaon, a realm led by Shamsuddin Ilyan Shah in Satgaon, and a realm led by Alauddin Ali Shah in Gauda.

  • Ilyas Shahi Dynasty

    (1342-1414 and 1435-1487)

    In 1352, Shamsuddin Ilyas Shah unified the three small sultanates in Bengal into a single governement, and proclaimed himself as the 'Shah of Bangalah'. His son Sikandar Shah defeated the Sultan of Delhi and secured recognition of Bengal's sovereignty after the Bengal Sultanate-Delhi Sultanate War. The Ilyas Shahi was interrupted in 1414 by a native uprising but was restrored by Nasiruddin Mahmud Shah in 1433.

  • Hussain Shahi Dynasty

    (1484-1538)

    The Bengal Sultanate's territory reached its greatest extent under Alauddin Hussain Shah, founder of the Hussain Shahi dynasty. The dynasty is regarded by several historians as a golden age in which a syncretic Bengali culture evolved including elements of Muslim and Hindu traditions.

  • Suri Empire

    (1539-1564)

    In the 16th century, the Mughal emperor Humayun was forced to take shelter in Persia as the conqueror Sher Shah Suri rampaged through the subcontinent. Bengal was brought under the control of the short-lived Suri Empire.

  • Karrani Dynasty

    (1564-1576)

    An Afghan dynasty was the last royal house of the Bengal Sultanate. The capital of the dynasty was Sonargaon. The dynasty also ruled parts of Bihar and Orissa. Its eastern boundary was formed by the Brahmaputra River.

  • Baro-Bhuyans

    (1576-1610)

    A confederation of twelve zamindar families resisted the expansion of the Mughal Empire during the 16th and 17th centuries. The zamindars included Muslims and Hindus. They were led by the Muslim Rajput chief Isa Khan.

  • Mughal Period

    Mughal Empire

    (1574-1717)

    The Mughal absorption of Bengal began with the Battle of Ghaghra in 1529, in which the Mughal army was led by the first Mughal emperor Babur.

  • Subedar Period

    (1574-1727)

    Subedars were the Mughal viceroys in Bengal. The Bengal Subah was part of a larger prosperous empire and shaped by imperial policies of pluralistic government. The Mughals built the provincial capital in Dhaka in 1610 with fortifications, gardens, tombs, palaces and mosques. Dhaka was also named in honour of Emperor Jahangir as Jahangirnagar.

  • Nawabs of Bengal

    (1717-1757)

    By the 18th century, Mughal Bengal became a quasi-independent country under the nominal rule of the emperor in Delhi. The subedar was elevated to the status of a hereditary Nawab Nazim. The Nawabs maintained de facto control of Bengal while minting coins in the name of the emperor in Delhi.

  • Nasiri Dynasty

    (1717-1740)

    The dynasty was founded by the first Nawab of Bengal Murshid Quli Khan. Its other rulers included Sarfaraz Khan and Shuja-ud-Din Muhammad Khan.

  • Afsar Dynasty

    (1740-1757)

    The dynasty was founded by Alivardi Khan. His grandson and successor Siraj-ud-daulah was the last independent Nawab of Bengal due to his defeat to British forces at the Battle of Plassey in 1757.
